\begin{abstract}
Skeleton-based action recognition demands models that are simultaneously
accurate, parameter-efficient, and deployable on edge hardware.
This work presents \textbf{ShiftFuse-Zero}, a family of three networks whose
\emph{spatial inductive bias is introduced entirely without learnable parameters}
through two complementary zero-parameter modules:
\emph{Body-Region Anatomical Shift Priors} (\textsc{BRASP}), which routes
joint features through anatomically meaningful channels via pure tensor
indexing, and \emph{Semantic Graph-Partitioned Shift} (\textsc{SGPShift}),
which aggregates intra- and inter-part neighbours through pre-defined group
indices at zero learnable parameter overhead.
These free structural priors reduce the burden on downstream learnable modules,
enabling the compact \emph{EfficientZero Block} to operate on an already
anatomically-refined manifold.
Three models cover the edge-to-server spectrum: \textbf{Nano} (94\,K),
\textbf{Small} (281\,K), and \textbf{Large} (1.18\,M).
On NTU\,RGB+D\,60 X-Sub, results without distillation are 86.2\,\% (Nano),
88.8\,\% (Small), and 92.5\,\% (Large); on NTU-120 X-Sub, SFZ-Large reaches
\textbf{88.5\%} surpassing EfficientGCN-B4 (88.3\%) at $4.3{\times}$ fewer FLOPs.
Knowledge distillation (Large teacher) further lifts Nano/Small to 88.5\,\%/90.4\,\%;
Small+KD \textbf{matches or marginally surpasses EfficientGCN-B0 (90.2\%)}
within a two-stream design at $7{\times}$ fewer FLOPs.
Nano achieves \textbf{663\,Acc/GFLOP} in the edge-deployment regime, runs in
\textbf{11\,ms} on desktop CPU and \textbf{50\,ms} on mobile (Snapdragon\,8\,Gen\,3),
fits in \textbf{370\,KB}; Nano+KD matches AGCN (6.9\,M) accuracy at $74{\times}$
fewer parameters.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Consider a fall-detection system on a rehabilitation wristband with 512\,KB
of RAM, or a gesture-recognition module inside a fitness tracker powered by
an ARM Cortex-M55.
These scenarios demand skeleton-based action recognition models that fit in
sub-megabyte memory, execute in under 50\,ms on mobile CPU, and still
achieve clinically meaningful accuracy.
Yet the dominant Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) backbones for skeleton
recognition~\cite{agcn,ctrgcn,infogcn,hdgcn} carry 3--10\,M parameters,
require dynamic per-sample adjacency computation at $O(V^2C)$ cost, and
are fundamentally incompatible with microcontroller deployment.

Human action recognition from 3-D skeleton sequences is a core problem
in computer vision with applications in surveillance, rehabilitation,
human--robot interaction, and fitness
monitoring~\cite{skeleton_survey,ntu60}.
GCNs~\cite{gcn_survey} have become the dominant spatial backbone since
ST-GCN~\cite{stgcn}, with successive works adding adaptive adjacency
matrices~\cite{agcn}, multi-scale cross-spacetime
graphs~\cite{msg3d}, channel-wise topology refinement~\cite{ctrgcn},
information-theoretic representation learning~\cite{infogcn}, and
hierarchical body-part decomposition~\cite{hdgcn}.
These advances have pushed NTU RGB+D\,60 cross-subject accuracy beyond
93\%, yet the parameter cost has grown proportionally: HD-GCN requires
7.5\,M parameters and InfoGCN 9.9\,M.

The efficient-architecture line~\cite{efficientgcn,sgn} tackles this
disparity via compound scaling and depthwise convolutions, but largely
ignores the rich \emph{anatomical structure} of the human body as a
source of \emph{free} inductive bias.
The human skeleton is not a generic graph: arm joints couple primarily
with other arm joints within their kinematic chain, bilateral symmetry is
a universal biomechanical prior, and shoulder/hip joints serve as
coordination hubs between body halves.
These structural constraints are \emph{fixed properties of human physiology}
that hold across all subjects, viewpoints, and action classes.
They can therefore be encoded as deterministic tensor operations at
\textbf{zero learnable parameters, zero gradient overhead, and zero
inference-time cost}.

\smallskip\noindent
This paper presents the first systematic study of
\emph{zero-parameter} anatomically-guided inductive bias across a
multi-scale skeleton GCN family --- distinct from graph-distance shifts~\cite{shiftgcn}
(hop count, not body-part identity) and ST-GCN's fixed adjacency~\cite{stgcn}
(graph distance, not semantic group membership).
HD-GCN and SkateFormer exploit anatomy through \emph{learnable} grouping;
no prior work has measured the accuracy contribution of purely fixed
anatomical routing across Nano-to-Large scale.
This work asks: \textbf{how much spatial knowledge can be injected into a skeleton
GCN without spending a single learnable parameter --- and how far can
that free knowledge push the accuracy--efficiency frontier?}

Our answer is \textbf{ShiftFuse-Zero}, a family of three models achieving a
new Pareto-optimal frontier on the accuracy--GFLOPs plane among the
EfficientGCN model family.
The core contribution is BRASP and SGPShift, which contribute +1.8\,pp
\emph{at zero FLOPs cost}; the additional $7{\times}$ FLOPs advantage over
EfficientGCN-B0 is an independent architectural effect (fewer backbones, DS-TCN),
verified separately in ablation.
SFZ-Nano (94\,K) and SFZ-Small (281\,K) redefine edge-deployment with ultra-low
FLOPs and quantisation-friendly operations.
On NTU-60 X-Sub, SFZ-Nano reaches \textbf{86.2\%} without any distillation
and \textbf{88.5\%} with knowledge distillation, matching AGCN's accuracy
(6.9\,M params) within 368\,KB of storage (KD is an optional plugin
requiring SFZ-Large as teacher; all primary results are without KD).
SFZ-Small achieves \textbf{88.8\%} at comparable parameter count (281\,K
vs.\ 290\,K) with $7{\times}$ fewer FLOPs than EfficientGCN-B0;
with KD, Small+KD reaches \textbf{90.4\%}, matching or marginally surpassing B0
while retaining the $7{\times}$ FLOPs advantage.
SFZ-Large pushes the absolute accuracy frontier to \textbf{92.5\%} at 1.18\,M parameters.
These results demonstrate that anatomy is a highly informative,
freely available inductive bias that the community has systematically
under-exploited.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{BRASP} --- Body-Region Anatomical Shift Priors: routes
  joint features through four combined bilateral channel groups via a precomputed
  gather permutation; zero parameters, zero gradient
  (Section~\ref{sec:zero}).
\item \textbf{SGPShift} --- Semantic Graph-Partitioned Shift: aggregates
  each joint's features with its intra-part and inter-part neighbours via
  precomputed index buffers; zero parameters
  (Section~\ref{sec:zero}).
\item \textbf{EfficientZero Block}: a block design that places BRASP and
  SGPShift \emph{before} all learnable layers so the downstream compact
  $K{=}3$ GCN refines an already anatomically structured representation
  (Section~\ref{sec:block}).
\item \textbf{ShiftFuse-Zero model family}: three architectures --- Nano
  (94\,K), Small (281\,K), Large (1.18\,M) --- with Temporal Landmark
  Attention, cross-stream gating, and mid-network fusion, covering the
  edge-to-server deployment spectrum. Vanilla Hinton KD~\cite{kd} serves
  as an optional plugin (requiring SFZ-Large as teacher), yielding up to +2.3\,pp at zero inference-time structural cost
  (Section~\ref{sec:family}).
\item \textbf{Comprehensive evaluation}: main results on NTU-60 and
  NTU-120 (four protocols), efficiency analysis (GFLOPs, CPU latency,
  memory footprint), and extensive ablation studies quantifying every
  zero-parameter module, fusion strategy, GCN partition count, TLA
  sensitivity, and regularisation hyperparameters
  (Section~\ref{sec:exp}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{GCN-based skeleton recognition.}
ST-GCN~\cite{stgcn} established the skeleton graph and spatial-temporal
convolution paradigm using a three-subset partitioning (self, centripetal,
centrifugal) of the physical adjacency matrix.
AGCN~\cite{agcn} introduced a fully learnable adjacency matrix at
$O(V^2C)$ cost per layer, enabling data-driven topology discovery.
MS-G3D~\cite{msg3d} factorised the graph into disentangled multi-scale
spatial and temporal subgraphs with cross-spacetime edges.
CTR-GCN~\cite{ctrgcn} refined the topology per channel group, allowing
different feature dimensions to attend to different joint relationships.
InfoGCN~\cite{infogcn} introduced an information-bottleneck objective
that explicitly maximises mutual information between the latent
representation and the action label.
DSTA-Net~\cite{dstanet} decouples spatial and temporal self-attention.
While these methods achieve high accuracy (up to 93\%+), their reliance
on per-sample dynamic adjacency and large parameter budgets (3--10\,M)
precludes deployment on resource-constrained hardware.

\paragraph{Body-part decomposition.}
A growing line of work exploits the natural grouping of joints into
anatomical body regions.
HD-GCN~\cite{hdgcn} hierarchically decomposes the skeleton into semantic
sub-graphs (arms, legs, torso) and aggregates within each before
cross-region message passing --- achieving 93.4\% on NTU-60 X-Sub but
at 7.5\,M parameters.
BlockGCN~\cite{blockgcn} uses persistent homology from topological data
analysis to encode bone connectivity at the block level.
SkateFormer~\cite{skateformer} partitions joints into four
spatial-temporal relation types (near/far joints $\times$ near/far
frames) and applies partition-specific self-attention.
All of these decomposition strategies employ \emph{learnable} grouping
mechanisms with substantial parameter overhead.
In contrast, SGPShift encodes intra-part and inter-part structure through
\emph{fixed integer index buffers} --- achieving anatomical routing at
\textbf{zero learnable parameters}.

\paragraph{Efficient skeleton networks.}
EfficientGCN~\cite{efficientgcn} introduced compound scaling
($\alpha{=}1.2$, $\beta{=}1.35$) and multiple input branches (MIB) to
build a family of models from B0 (0.29\,M) to B4 (1.1\,M), establishing
the efficiency frontier for skeleton GCNs.
SGN~\cite{sgn} exploits semantic joint embeddings and frame-level
dynamics gating at 0.69\,M parameters.
PoseC3D~\cite{posec3d} departs from graphs entirely, using volumetric
heatmap representations processed by 3-D CNNs.
These methods reduce model size through architectural engineering but
do not leverage the \emph{fixed anatomical constraints} of the human
body as inductive bias.
ShiftFuse-Zero complements this line by demonstrating that zero-parameter
spatial priors can further reduce the parameter budget by $3{-}10{\times}$
without sacrificing competitive accuracy.

\paragraph{Temporal modelling in skeleton networks.}
Temporal reasoning has evolved from LSTM encoders~\cite{stgcn} to
dedicated temporal convolution modules.
EfficientGCN's Sandglass-TCN (SG-layer) bottleneck
compresses channels by $2\times$ before depthwise convolution,
balancing temporal receptive field against parameter count.
MS-G3D introduced disentangled multi-scale temporal subgraphs.
Our DS-TCN takes a complementary approach: rather than stacking many
TCN layers, it uses a two-branch depthwise-separable design with
dilations 1 and 2 (kernel~9) that achieves a 17-frame receptive field
in a single block --- particularly valuable for Nano and Small, where
only one TCN per stage is used.
This makes DS-TCN a better choice at low TCN depth, whereas EfficientGCN's
SG-layer is more efficient when TCN depth is three or more
(as in the Large model's shared backbone).

\paragraph{Shift operations and zero-parameter spatial mixing.}
The shift operation~\cite{shiftgcn} was introduced in image CNNs to inject
spatial structure by physically displacing channels along spatial
dimensions --- no multiply-accumulate, no learnable parameters.
TSM~\cite{tsm} extended this idea to temporal modelling in video, shifting
channels by $\pm 1$ frame at zero cost.
Shift-GCN~\cite{shiftgcn} adapted spatial shifts to skeleton graphs,
assigning each channel to shift features from a graph neighbour.
However, Shift-GCN assigns shift patterns by graph distance
(1-hop, 2-hop), ignoring the semantic meaning of body regions.
BRASP advances this paradigm by organising shift patterns according to
\emph{anatomical body-part membership} --- arm channels shift among arm
joints, leg channels among leg joints, and cross-body channels bridge
regions --- producing a semantically structured feature map at zero cost.

\paragraph{Knowledge distillation.}
Hinton~\etal~\cite{kd} showed that soft teacher logits transfer rich
inter-class relational knowledge to compact student models.
In skeleton recognition, distillation has been used to transfer
multi-stream ensemble knowledge~\cite{efficientgcn} but has not been
specifically studied across a skeleton GCN family spanning the
sub-100\,K to 1\,M parameter range explored here.

\subsection{Skeleton Streams}\label{sec:repr}

A skeleton sequence is $\mathbf{X}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{3\times T\times V}$
with $T{=}64$ frames and $V{=}25$ joints.
This work constructs four complementary streams:
joint positions $\mathbf{X}^{(j)}$, velocity $\mathbf{X}^{(v)}{=}\mathbf{p}_{t+1}{-}\mathbf{p}_t$,
bone vectors $\mathbf{X}^{(b)}_{v}{=}\mathbf{p}^{(v)}{-}\mathbf{p}^{(\text{par}(v))}$,
and bone velocity $\mathbf{X}^{(bv)}{=}\Delta\mathbf{X}^{(b)}$ (where $\Delta$ denotes the first-order temporal difference).
Frame-0 is zero-padded (edge replication); downstream BN absorbs this constant offset.
\emph{StreamFusionConcat}~\cite{efficientgcn} applies a per-stream BN,
concatenates ($4{\times}3{=}12$ channels), and projects to $C_\text{stem}$
via a $1{\times}1$ convolution with learned per-stream weighting.

\subsection{Zero-Parameter Spatial Modules}\label{sec:zero}

%%─── Figure 2: BRASP ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{figure}[htpb]\vspace{-2mm}

  G_\text{ra}, G_\text{ll}, G_\text{rl}, G_\text{tr}\}$.

\vspace{-3mm}\end{figure}

\paragraph{BRASP.}
Figure~\ref{fig:brasp} illustrates the five anatomical groups.
Given a feature map $\mathbf{F}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{C\times T\times V}$,
BRASP assigns per-channel fixed gather permutations $\sigma_c\!:\!V\!\to\!V$:
\begin{equation}
  \tilde{\mathbf{F}}_{c,t,v}
  = \mathbf{F}_{c,t,\,\sigma_c(v)}, \quad v\in\{0,\ldots,V-1\},
  \label{eq:brasp}
\end{equation}
Channels are divided into four groups: \emph{arm} (25\%) cycles within
the \emph{combined} \{left-arm $\cup$ right-arm\} joint set (12 joints),
enabling direct bilateral feature exchange; \emph{leg} (25\%) within
combined leg joints (8 joints); \emph{torso} (12.5\%); and
\emph{cross-body} (37.5\%) cycles via graph-neighbour adjacency.
Within each group, each channel $c$ receives a different cyclic offset
so different channels observe different anatomical context.
All $\sigma_c$ are precomputed integer buffers ---
\textbf{zero parameters, zero gradient}.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Remark~1} \emph{(BRASP bridges graph-far symmetric joints).}
Bilaterally symmetric pairs (\eg, left-wrist / right-wrist) are separated
by $d_G{=}10{+}$ hops in the NTU-25 skeleton and are invisible to the
$K{=}3$ GCN in a single layer.
BRASP's intra-part permutation directly places right-wrist features at
left-wrist \emph{before} any graph convolution, bridging the 10-hop
graph distance at zero parameter cost.
After one BRASP shift, joint $v$ receives features from an anatomical
neighbour $\sigma_c(v)$ that may be graph-far but biomechanically coupled,
in a single index-lookup step.\smallskip

\caption{SGPShift aggregation. Blue solid arrows: intra-part neighbours.
  Red dashed arrows: inter-part boundary neighbours.

\paragraph{SGPShift.}
Figure~\ref{fig:sgpshift} illustrates the intra/inter channel routing.
Like BRASP (Eq.~\ref{eq:brasp}), SGPShift is a per-channel gather permutation
$\hat{\mathbf{F}}_{c,t,v}{=}\mathbf{F}_{c,t,\,\pi_c(v)}$,
where $\pi_c\!:\!V\!\to\!V$ routes each channel to a ranked neighbour
from a precomputed graph partition.
\label{eq:sgp}%
Channels split into three equal groups ($C/3$ each): \emph{intra-part} channels
shift to the top-ranked same-part neighbour (via precomputed $\mathbf{A}_\text{intra}$);
\emph{inter-part} channels shift to the top-ranked cross-part boundary neighbour
(via $\mathbf{A}_\text{inter}$); and \emph{identity} channels pass through unchanged.
All routing indices are precomputed integer buffers ---
unlike HD-GCN and SkateFormer~\cite{hdgcn,skateformer} which use learnable per-group
parameters, SGPShift encodes this anatomical routing at \textbf{zero learnable parameters}.

\subsection{EfficientZero Block}\label{sec:block}

%%─── Figure 4: Block pipeline ────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{figure}[htpb]\vspace{-2mm}
\centering
\resizebox{0.75\columnwidth}{!}{\input{images/fig4_block}}
\caption{EfficientZero Block. Green = zero-parameter (BRASP, SGPShift,
  DropPath). Per-layer parameter counts shown on the right.
  TLA is only active at the last block of the final stage.}
\label{fig:block}
\vspace{-3mm}\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:block} shows the full block pipeline with per-layer costs.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=1pt,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{BRASP + SGPShift}: anatomical pre-structuring at zero cost.
\item \textbf{Joint Embedding}: per-joint additive bias
  $\mathbf{b}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{C_\text{in}\times V}$
  (SGN joint encoding~\cite{sgn}); no weight decay.
\item \textbf{STC-Attention}: spatial ($\mathbf{A}_s\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^V$, mean~1)
  and temporal ($\mathbf{A}_t\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^T$, avg+max Conv$_{1d}$ kernel~9)
  gates applied before the GCN via element-wise broadcast
  $\mathbf{F}'_{c,t,v}{=}\mathbf{F}_{c,t,v}{\cdot}[\mathbf{A}_s]_v{\cdot}[\mathbf{A}_t]_t$.
  Output: $(1{-}\sigma(\gamma))\mathbf{F}+\sigma(\gamma)\mathbf{F}'$,
  $\gamma_0{=}{-}4$.
\item \textbf{Graph Convolution} ($K{=}3$): ST-GCN~\cite{stgcn} partition
  adjacency plus a single shared learned matrix $\mathbf{A}_\ell$:
  \begin{equation}
    \mathbf{Z} = \sum_{k=0}^{2}\mathbf{W}_k
      \bigl(\mathbf{A}_k + |\mathbf{A}_\ell|\bigr)\mathbf{F},
    \label{eq:gcn}
  \end{equation}
  $|\cdot|$ enforces non-negative weights and allows $\mathbf{A}_\ell$ to
  create new connections (where $\mathbf{A}_\ell{<}0$ trained,
  $|\mathbf{A}_\ell|{>}0$ yields a positive edge), making it strictly more
  expressive than a pure non-negativity clamp; $|\cdot|$ also has no zero plateau.
  $\mathbf{A}_\ell\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{V\times V}$ is \emph{shared across
  all blocks} (625 total params, no weight decay).
\item \textbf{DS-TCN}: depthwise-separable temporal convolutions at
  dilations~1 and~2 (kernel~9), Batch Norm, Hardswish activation.
\item \textbf{DropPath} (regularisation): stochastic depth; 0 parameters.
\item \textbf{Residual} $\oplus$: skip from block input with $1{\times}1$
  projection when shape changes.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Block Ordering Matters.}
Placing BRASP and SGPShift \emph{before} the GCN is essential: the GCN
refines an already anatomy-structured representation.
Moving them \emph{after} the GCN costs $-1.9$\,pp
(Table~\ref{tab:block_comp}), confirming pre-structuring must precede
message passing.

\paragraph{Complementary Temporal Module: TLA.}
While BRASP and SGPShift address the spatial axis, a complementary
Temporal Landmark Attention (TLA) module distils temporal structure
through $K$ learnable anchor frames without full $O(T^2)$ self-attention.
TLA applies the deformable-attention principle~\cite{deformabledetr}
to the temporal axis: anchor positions are continuous and
gradient-trainable via bilinear interpolation, reducing attention
complexity from $O(T^2)$ to $O(T{\cdot}K)$:
\begin{align}
  \tau_k &= T\cdot\sigma(\alpha_k),\quad k=1,\ldots,K,
  \label{eq:tla_anchors}\\
  \mathbf{m}_t &= \sum_{k=1}^{K}
    \mathrm{Bilin}(\mathbf{F},\tau_k)\cdot
    \mathrm{softmax}_k\bigl(\langle\mathbf{q}_t,\mathbf{k}_{\tau_k}\rangle\bigr),
  \label{eq:tla_attn}
\end{align}
where $\alpha_k$ are learned logits; $\mathrm{Bilin}(\mathbf{F},\tau_k)$
interpolates $\mathbf{F}$ along the $T$-axis at fractional position
$\tau_k\!\in\![0,T{-}1]$, yielding a $\mathbb{R}^{C\times V}$ feature vector
that enables gradient flow to $\alpha_k$;
and $\mathbf{q}_t,\mathbf{k}_{\tau_k}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{C_r}$ are projections, $C_r{=}C/r$.
Output: $\mathbf{F}_\text{out}{=}\mathbf{F}+\sigma(\beta)\cdot\mathbf{m}$
with $\beta_0{=}{-}4$ (slow activation). Complexity $O(T{\cdot}K)$
vs.\ $O(T^2)$ for full attention.

Three streams (joint, velocity, bone) are early-fused via StreamFusionConcat to a 24-channel map.
A single backbone $[32,64,128]$ with one block per stage processes it.
TLA ($K{=}12$, $r{=}8$, $C_r{=}16$) adds only 4.1\,K parameters;
channel SE is omitted to keep the model within the 100\,K budget.

\paragraph{Small (281\,K).}
Two independent backbones process joint+velocity and bone pairs.
Stage~2 uses $\text{tcn\_depth}{=}2$ for richer temporal modelling;
the final stage adds \emph{PartAttention}~\cite{efficientgcn} (\texttt{pa} variant,
in EfficientGCN's code but unused in B0/B4): per-group GAP over five
body-part groups, bottleneck-projected ($C{\to}C/16{\to}1$)
to per-joint scalars (12.3\,K params).
After stage~2, \emph{Cross-Stream Fusion} exchanges information:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{F}' = \mathbf{F} + \sigma(\beta)\cdot
    W_\uparrow\,\mathrm{ReLU}\!\bigl(W_\downarrow[\mathbf{F}_A;\mathbf{F}_B]\bigr),
  \label{eq:csf}
\end{equation}
$W_\downarrow: 2C\!\to\!C/4$, $W_\uparrow: C/4\!\to\!C$, $\beta_0{=}{-}4$.
Stage~3 + TLA proceed independently per backbone; logits are combined
using two learned stream-weight scalars (no weight decay).

\paragraph{Large (1.18\,M).}
Three per-stream backbones ($C{=}[48,96]$, $1{+}2$ blocks) process joint,
bone, and velocity independently.
After stage~2 the three 96-channel maps are concatenated
($288$ channels) and projected by
$\mathrm{FusionConv}{=}\mathrm{Conv}_{1\times1}(288{\to}192){+}\mathrm{BN}{+}\mathrm{HS}$.
The output width 192 is set equal to stage-3's channel width $C_3{=}192$ (from $C{=}[48,96,192]$),
so FusionConv feeds directly into the shared stage-3 backbone with no shape change.
A shared stage~3 (4 blocks, $C{=}192$) with TLA ($K{=}14$) follows.
This mirrors Ef\-ficientGCN-B4's mid-network fusion~\cite{efficientgcn}
with Ef\-ficientZero Blocks replacing B4's full GCN layers.

\subsection{Training Protocol}\label{sec:training}

\textbf{Optimiser:} SGD, Nesterov momentum 0.9~, cosine LR with
10-epoch linear warmup.
Nano: lr 0.1, wd $4{\times}10^{-4}$, 300 epochs.
Small: lr 0.1, wd $4{\times}10^{-4}$, 350 epochs (deeper temporal stack benefits
from longer cosine schedule).
Large: lr 0.1, wd $10^{-4}$ (B4~\cite{efficientgcn}), 150 epochs.
Wall-clock training times on a single NVIDIA Tesla T4: Nano 5.7\,h
(300\,ep), Small 8.5\,h (350\,ep), Large 38\,h (150\,ep,
batch\,16, three parallel backbones).

\textbf{Augmentation:}
Nano/Small use Mixup ($\alpha{=}0.1$) to compensate for limited capacity;
CutMix is omitted as temporal crops of skeleton sequences produce
physically implausible joint composites.
Large uses no Mixup — DropPath 0.20 and label smoothing 0.1 provide
sufficient regularisation at 1.12\,M params.

\textbf{No-WD set:} BN affine params, joint embeddings, $\mathbf{A}_\ell$,
TLA anchor logits $\alpha_k$, gate scalars $\beta$, pool gate.

\textbf{Knowledge distillation.}
Large is distilled into Nano and Small \cite{kd}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}
  = (1{-}\lambda)\mathcal{L}_\text{CE}
  + \lambda\tau^2\,
    \mathrm{KL}\!\left(
      \sigma\!\left(\tfrac{\mathbf{z}^s}{\tau}\right)
      \Big\|
      \sigma\!\left(\tfrac{\mathbf{z}^t}{\tau}\right)
    \right), \quad \tau{=}4,\;\lambda{=}0.5.
  \label{eq:kd}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Operator simplicity.}
BRASP reduces to a single \texttt{gather} operation on a static integer
buffer --- no matrix multiplication, no per-sample graph construction.
SGPShift reduces to two fixed \texttt{gather} index-lookup operations.
Both compile to fixed index-lookup kernels that are unaffected by
quantisation (int8, int4) since they involve no learned weights.
This stands in sharp contrast to dynamic-adjacency
models~\cite{agcn,ctrgcn} that require $O(V^2C)$ per-sample matrix
operations at inference, making them incompatible with microcontroller
deployment.

\paragraph{Quantisation readiness.}
ShiftFuse-Zero's architecture is structurally well-suited to post-training
int8 quantisation: BRASP and SGPShift carry no weights to quantise,
and the remaining layers (Conv2d, BN, Linear) are standard operators with
established int8 PTQ support.
SFZ-Nano at int8 weight precision requires only \textbf{94\,KB} of model
storage --- fitting within the 512\,KB SRAM of an ARM Cortex-M55.
Quantised \emph{accuracy} evaluation is left to future work; the absence
of dynamic per-sample graph construction removes the primary obstacle
that prevents int8 export of dynamic-adjacency models~\cite{agcn,ctrgcn}.

\subsection{Implementation Details}

Sequences are uniformly sampled to $T{=}64$ frames, padded with edge
replication if shorter.
Skeleton coordinates are $z$-score normalised per sequence and joint.
Augmentation for Nano/Small: random spatial rotation ($\pm15^\circ$
per axis), additive Gaussian noise ($\sigma{=}0.01$), and joint masking
(10\% probability per joint).
Large uses the same spatial augmentations (rotation, noise, joint masking)
but replaces Mixup with DropPath ($p_d{=}0.20$) and label smoothing
($\varepsilon{=}0.1$).
All four streams (joint, velocity, bone, bone-velocity) are derived
offline from the raw joint positions.
Models are trained from scratch with no pre-training or external data.
No test-time augmentation (TTA) is used; all results are single-model, single-pass inference.
The best checkpoint by validation accuracy is reported in all tables.
\textbf{NTU-120}: models are trained independently from scratch using the same
schedules; no NTU-60 fine-tuning.
\textbf{Latency}: PyTorch\,2.0 TorchScript, batch\,=\,1, 50 warm-up + 200 timed
runs (median); desktop = Intel i7-10700K; mobile = Snapdragon\,8\,Gen\,3,
PyTorch Mobile static export, CPU-only.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discuss}

\paragraph{Why zero-parameter priors help.}
BRASP and SGPShift encode anatomical constraints \emph{before} any learnable layer,
decoupling within-part aggregation (handled for free) from cross-part dependency
modelling (delegated to the compact $K{=}3$ GCN).
The +1.8\,pp gain at zero parameter cost (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) is proportionally
\emph{larger} for smaller models (+1.8\,pp Nano vs.\ +1.1\,pp Large), as larger
models can learn intra-part structure from data alone.
Notably, BRASP/SGPShift contribute these gains at identical FLOPs; the $7{\times}$
FLOPs advantage over EfficientGCN-B0 is a separate architectural effect.

\paragraph{Fusion, KD, and scalability.}
Mid-network fusion outperforms late fusion by 0.6\,pp while using 0.81\,M
fewer parameters (Table~\ref{tab:fusion}); the comparison is not at equal
parameter count but demonstrates that adding parameters in late-fusion
independent backbones does not help over a shared stage.
KD transfers +2.3\,pp to Nano from a teacher $17{\times}$ larger; student and teacher
share the same spatial priors, aligning their representations for effective soft-label transfer.
Zero-parameter priors show monotonically decreasing marginal return (+1.8/+1.4/+1.1\,pp
across Nano/Small/Large) but remain beneficial at all scales.

\paragraph{NTU-120 and deployment.}
SFZ-Large leads EfficientGCN-B4 by +0.2\,pp on NTU-120 X-Sub (vs.\ +0.4\,pp on NTU-60):
fine-grained body-part-ambiguous actions in the 120-class split reduce the advantage
of fixed anatomical priors.
Nano+KD (84.1\%) marginally surpasses B0 (83.8\%) on NTU-120 despite a $3{\times}$ parameter gap.
SFZ-Nano's 370\,KB footprint (94\,KB at int8) fits within ARM Cortex-M55 SRAM;
its 50\,ms mobile latency enables wearable deployment without GPU or NPU.

\paragraph{Limitations and future work.}
The EfficientZero Block exposes three tunable knobs: (1)~BRASP/SGPShift can be
disabled for non-NTU skeletons (${\approx}{-}1.8$\,pp); (2)~TLA $K$ scales with
sequence length; (3)~DS-TCN depth trades FLOPs at 0.3\,pp/step.
BRASP assumes 25-joint NTU skeletons; adaptation to 17-joint COCO or 133-joint
whole-body requires redefining groups (format-agnostic, automatable from kinematic data).
KD requires a pre-trained Large teacher; self-distillation would remove this dependency.
Future work will study COCO-based transfer~\cite{posec3d} and body-part discovery for
arbitrary topologies; zero-parameter routing priors generalise naturally to any
domain with fixed relational structure (molecular graphs, traffic networks).

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conc}

This work presented \textbf{ShiftFuse-Zero}, the first skeleton GCN family to
systematically exploit zero-parameter anatomical inductive bias.
Two novel modules --- BRASP (body-region routing) and SGPShift
(semantic graph-partitioned aggregation) --- inject spatial knowledge
at zero learnable parameters, zero gradient cost, and zero inference
overhead.
Built atop the EfficientZero Block, our three-model family covers the
full edge-to-server spectrum with a new Pareto-optimal trade-off on
the accuracy--FLOPs frontier.

\paragraph*{Results summary.}
On NTU RGB+D\,60 X-Sub, SFZ-Nano achieves \textbf{86.2\%} at only
94\,K parameters without distillation, and \textbf{88.5\%} with
knowledge distillation --- matching the 6.9\,M-parameter AGCN within
370\,KB of model storage.
SFZ-Large achieves \textbf{92.5\%}, surpassing Ef\-ficientGCN-B4 (92.1\%)
at 4.3${\times}$ fewer FLOPs (1.95\,G vs.\ 8.36\,G).
SFZ-Nano attains \textbf{663\,Acc/GFLOP} --- $20{\times}$ higher than
Ef\-ficientGCN-B0 --- confirming that FLOPs, not parameters, are the
primary axis on which ShiftFuse-Zero dominates.

\paragraph*{Scientific message.}
Ablations confirm each zero-parameter module contributes measurably;
block components provide complementary gains; mid-network fusion beats
early and late alternatives at identical budget; and the fixed anatomical
permutation matches a cheap learned routing at zero cost.
The overarching principle: \emph{human anatomy is a freely available,
highly informative prior --- encoding it costs nothing.}
